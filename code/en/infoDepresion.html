Depression

What is depression?

Clinical depression is a serious and common illness that affects us physically and mentally in our way of feeling and thinking.

Depression can make us want to withdraw from our family, friends, work, and school. It can also cause anxiety, loss of sleep, loss of appetite, and lack of interest or pleasure in doing different activities. “Juan and I had imagined for a long time wonderful plans to visit our families and see our friends again. However, at that time taking a trip was the last thing I wanted to do. I felt so tired! Some mornings I couldn't even get out of bed." “I woke up every night before dawn. I worried about my baby, about my mom, about my job. Then when I managed to get one, I couldn't even focus on what I was doing." “My family and friends realized that "I was no longer the same". I couldn't enjoy the things I liked, like dancing, watching a movie, and even making love. I didn't understand what was happening to me, and I was worried that I would feel like this forever!" "I had huge problems sleeping, I didn't do my job well, I lost my taste for food." “After giving birth to my baby, I thought I would be happy, but I felt worse and worse. I was very sad, I felt guilty for not enjoying my new baby.” Is this what is happening to you? Is it happening to someone you know?

Did you know that all of these people could be experiencing clinical depression?

Almost all of us have felt immense sadness at some point in our lives. This is normal.

Almost all of us have felt immense sadness at some point in our lives. This is normal. But if this sadness or depressive attitude continues for more than two weeks, help should be sought. Feeling sadness is normal, being clinically depressed is not. Clinical depression is not simply an anguish, it is also a permanent sadness or melancholy. It leads us to feel useless, hopeless; sometimes, we may want to give up. Clinical depression causes loss of pleasure in daily life, strain at work and in relationships, aggravates medical conditions and can even lead to suicide. With help that dark cloud will disappear and you will see the blue sky again. The sun can and should shine again for you. Depression is a treatable illness. With help and support you can heal. Clinical depression can be treated. We can take back control of our lives. We don't need to live feeling anxious, sad and irritable. We don't need to spend sleepless nights either! Anyone experiencing depressive symptoms should be diagnosed and treated. There are different ways to control the different forms of depression. The most common, but the most severe is clinical depression.

Who can be affected by clinical depression?

It can affect anyone, of any economic level and at any age. Mental illnesses do not discriminate! They affect rich and poor, black and white, Hispanic/Latino and Asian, the elderly and children.

However, when Hispanics/Latinos experience mental health problems, we must face in addition to our illness, numerous barriers to receiving adequate care and treatment. Among the most common barriers are socio-economic differences, inequality in access to health coverage, lack of sociocultural sensitivity in the patient-provider relationship, and lack of health insurance. That is why it is important that we are well informed about our rights to be respected and cared for with dignity and free from prejudice or stigma.

What are the factors that contribute to clinical depression?

The reasons for falling into a depressive state are very varied. Some people fall into clinical depression when they experience an emotional event in their lives, for example, when we lose our job or end an important love relationship. Other people may be affected as a result of a combination of factors and events: When we migrate and leave our dearest family and friends behind in our country; when we feel a lack of ability to communicate; when we find it difficult to understand and integrate into a new culture. However, there are people who can become depressed without warning.

Some factors that can contribute to clinical depression include:

Sex, women suffer twice more from depression than men. Although the reasons are not clear, there are genetic and hormonal differences that can contribute to depression.

Family history, when a member of your family suffers from severe depression, there are twice the chances of acquiring it. Although it can occur in people who do not have relatives with depression.

Use of certain medicines, some prescription and non-prescription medicines can cause clinical depression.

Changes or difficulties in life, such as divorce, retirement, death of a loved one, job loss, change of country and lifestyle, increased pressures at work or increase in poverty.

Feelings of losing control over our lives. Those people who often feel out of control and spend a lot of time regretting it are more likely to develop major depression.

Presence of other diseases such as Alzheimer's, cancer, diabetes, heart conditions, hormonal disorders, Parkinson's disease or thrombosis. As well as other mental disorders such as anxiety and eating disorders.

Alcohol or drug abuse, when you have problems with alcohol and other drug use, you are more likely to develop major depression.

What are the symptoms of clinical depression?

There are several, however, the symptoms do not appear in the same way in all people. In some people, only a few may appear, while in others, many of them may appear.

Make an appointment with your doctor right away if: You have had five or more of these symptoms for more than two weeks; The symptoms are affecting your daily life; He has contemplated the possibility of suicide. When you go to see your doctor, you can take your answers to this questionnaire with you. You can also ask to be screened for depression. These tests are available nationwide, free, quick, and easy to do. To do it you do not need to give your name, they are confidential. Don't be embarrassed to seek help. It is always healthier and more advisable to talk to someone than to try to find relief in alcohol or drugs. In addition, this practice can further aggravate your symptoms of depression.
